3. Analytic Geometry

  • In Chapter 2, we studied vectors, vector spaces, and linear mappings at a general but abstract level.

  • In this chapter, we will add some geometric interpretation and intuition to all of these concepts.

    • In particular, we will look at geometric vectors and compute their lengths and distances or angles between two vectors.

3.1 Norms

  • Norm : A norm on a vector space $V$ is a function, which assigns each vector $x$ its length $‖x‖ ∈R$

    $$ ‖x‖_k:= \sqrt{∑_i^n} x_i^k $$


3.2 Inner Products

  • Puropose : is to determine wheter vectors are orthogonal to each other*

  • Dot product : particular type of inner product

$$ x^Ty=∑_i=x_i y_i $$

3.2.2 General Inner Products

  • Bilinear mapping : is a mapping with two arguments and it is linear in each argument
    • symmetric : $Ω(x,y) = Ω(y,x)$
    • positive definite : $∀x∈V: Ω(x,x)>0$
    • Inner Product : Let $V$ be a vector space and $Ω :V×V→R$ be a bilinear mapping that takes two vectors and maps them onto a real number. Then a positive definite, symmetric bilinear mapping $Ω :V×V→R$ is called an inner product on $V$. We typically write $〈x,y〉$ instead of $Ω(x,y)$.

3.2.3 Symmetric, Positive Definite Matrices

  • Symmetric Postivie Definite Matrix

$$ ∀x∈V:x^TAx>0 $$


3.3 Lengths and Distances

  • Inner Product and Norm : any inner product induces a norm

$$ ‖x‖:= \sqrt{〈x,x〉} $$

  • Distance : Consider an inner product space (V,〈·,·〉). Then

$$ d(x,y) :=‖x−y‖= \sqrt{〈x−y,x−y〉} $$

  • Euclidean Distance : If we use the dotdistanceproduct as the inner product, then the distance is calledEuclidean distance
  • Metric : The mapping below is called a metric

$$ d:V×V→R \ (x,y)\mapsto d(x,y) $$


3.4 Angles and Orthogonality

  • Angle : inner products also capture the geometry of a vector space by defining the angle $ω$ between two vectors. The angle between two vectors tells us how similar their orientations are.

$$ cos ω = \frac{<x,y>}{‖x‖‖y‖} = \frac{<x,y>}{\sqrt{<x,x><y,y>}} $$

  • Orthogonality : Two vectors $x$ and $y$ are orthogonal if and only if $〈x,y〉= 0$, and we write $x⊥y$.
  • Orthonormal : If additionally $‖x‖= 1 =‖y‖$ ,i.e., the vectors are unit vectors, then $x$ and $y$ are orthonormal.
  • Orthogonal Matrix : A square matrix $A∈R^{n×n}$ is an orthogonal matrix if and only if its columns are orthonormal so that $AA^T = I=A^TA$ which implies that $A^{−1}=A^T$, i.e., the inverse is obtained by simply transposing the matrix..

3.5 Orthonormal Basis

  • Orthonormal Basis : Consider an n-dimensional vector space V and a basis {b1,…,bn} of V. If equation below is satisfied then the basis is called an orthonormal basis

    $$ <b_i,b_j>=0 \ <b_i, b_i>=1 $$

  • Gram-Schmidt process :

    • Assume we are given a set $[{b_1,…, b_n}]$ of non-orthogonal and unnormalized basis vectors.

    • We concatenate them into a matrix $B=[ b_1 ,…, b_n]$

    • apply Gaussian elimination to the augmented matrix (Section 2.3.2) $[ BB^T | B]$ to obtain an orthonormal basis.

    • This constructive way to iteratively build an orthonormal basis is called the Gram-Schmidt process (Strang, 2003)


3.6 Orthogonal Complement

  • Orthogonal Complement $U^⊥$ : Consider a vector space $V$ and its subspace $U$, $U^⊥$ is a subspace of $V$ and contains all vectors in $V$ that are orthogonal to every vector in $U$

3.7 Inner Product of Functions

  • Thus far, we looked at properties of inner products of finite-dimensional vectors.
  • The concept of an inner product can be generalized to vectors with an infinite number of entries (countably infinite) and also continuous-valued functions (uncountably infinite).
  • An inner product of two functions $u:R→R$ and $v:R→R$ can be defined as the definite integral

$$ 〈u,v〉 := ∫_b^a u(x)v(x)dx $$

  • Example 3.9 : If we choose $u=sin(x)$ and $v= cos(x)$, the integrand $f(x)=u(x)v(x)$ is odd, i.e., $f(−x) =−f(x)$. Therefore, the integral with limits $a=−π,b=π$ of this product evaluates to 0. Therefore, sin and cos are orthogonal functions.

3.8 Orthogonal Projections

  • We can project the original high-dimensional data onto a lower-dimensional feature space and work in this lower-dimensional space to learn moreabout the dataset and extract relevant patterns.
  • Projection : Let $V$ be a vector space and $U⊆V$ a subspace of $V$. A linear mapping $π:V→U$ is called a projection if $π^2=π◦π=π$.

3.8.1 Projection onto One-Dimensional Subspaces (Lines)

  • In 3.8.1, authors introduce how to find a projection point $π_U(x)$ onto 1D subspace $U$ and the projection matrix $P_π$

  • EX3.10) The application of $P_π$ to $π_U(x)$ does not change anything (it’s already projected onto $U$) => above definition

  • CH4) $π_U(x)$ is an eigenvector of $P_π$, and the corresponding eigenvalue is 1.

3.8.2 Projection onto General Subspaces

  • In 3.8.2, we look at orthogonal projections of vectors $x∈R_n$ onto lower-dimensional subspaces $U⊆R_n$ with $dim(U) =m >1$.

3.8.3 Gram Schmidt Orthogonalization

  • The Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization : iteratively Gram-Schmidt orthogonalization constructs an orthogonal basis $(u_1,…,u_n)$ from any basis $(b_1,…,b_n)$ of $V$ as follows:

$$ u_1:=b_1 $$

$$ u_k:=b_k−π_{span}(u_1,…,u_{k−1})(b_k) $$

  • $b_k$ is projected onto the subspace spanned by the first $k−1$ constructed orthogonal vectors $u_1,…,u_{k−1}$.
  • This projection is then subtracted from $b_k$ and yields a vector $u_k$ that is orthogonal to the (k−1)-dimensional subspace spanned by $u_1,…,u_{k−1}$.
  • Repeating this procedure for all $n$ basis vectors $b_1,…,b_n$ yields an orthogonal basis $(u_1,…,u_n)$ of $V$.
  • If we normalize the $u_k$, we obtain an ONB where $‖u_k‖= 1$ for $k= 1,…,n$.

3.9 Rotations

  • Rotation : is a linear mapping (more specifically, an automorphism of Euclidean vector space) that rotates a plane by an angle $θ$ about the origin.

3.9.2 Rotations in $\mathbb{R}^2$

  • Consider the standard basis $e_1, e_2$, we aim to rotate this coordinate system by an angle $\theta$

$$ e_1= \begin{bmatrix} 1 \\ 0 \end{bmatrix},e_2= \begin{bmatrix} 0 \\ 1 \end{bmatrix} $$

  • Rotations $Φ$ are linear mappings so that we can express them by a rotation matrix $R(θ)$ and we obtain

$$ Φ(e_1)= \begin{bmatrix} cosθ \\ sinθ \end{bmatrix},Φ(e_2) = \begin{bmatrix} -sinθ \\ cosθ \end{bmatrix} $$

  • Therefore, the rotation matrix that performs the basis change into the rotated coordinates $R(θ)$ is given as :

$$ R(\theta) = [Φ(e_1) \ Φ(e_2)] = \begin{bmatrix} cosθ \ -sinθ \\ sinθ \ cosθ \end{bmatrix} $$
